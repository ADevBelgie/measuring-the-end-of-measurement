# Measuring the End of Measurement
### Benchmark half-life, task horizons, and the timeline to cognitive supersession

**A brief synthesized from a conversation, July 2026**
*Status: informal synthesis. Point estimates are held loosely and are most useful as a structure for updating, not as forecasts to be quoted. Prose is deliberately compressed; if this document is ever promoted beyond informal synthesis, unpacking is the first job.*

*Disclosure: a participant in this synthesis is Arthur Devresse, co-author of the April 2026 strategic brief that supplied this document's seed hypothesis (the benchmark half-life). Citations of that brief are therefore self-citation; the mid-2026 verification of its claims was performed against independent sources (Scale AI, ARC Prize Foundation, Forrester, Gartner, Stanford, and others listed below) and does not route through the brief or its authors.*

---

## Executive summary

There is no benchmark for "AI supersedes humans on all cognitive tasks," and two barriers jointly prevent one: coverage (the task set is open-ended, so any benchmark is a contestable sample of what cognition is) and the evaluator ceiling (for most frontier tasks, ground truth routes through human judgment, which stops being a valid instrument near and past the frontier). Reality-graded domains — formal proof, resolved prediction, experimental outcome — escape the second barrier and can certify superhumanity locally, but their verdicts are slow, confounded, and domain-bound: they measure peaks, not coverage. This brief argues that the endpoint will therefore not be announced by a score but by a silence — the moment well-motivated designers can no longer construct any task that is easy for humans and hard for AI — a criterion this brief treats as a leading, cost-relative proxy for supersession rather than its definition (§1). Working backward from that criterion, and forward from the measurable trends (task time-horizons, benchmark half-lives, capability-vs-deployment gaps), we sketch a staged timeline: superhuman performance across verifiable cognitive domains plausibly by the late 2020s, most economically relevant cognition by roughly 2029–2031, and the full "silence" with a mode in the early-to-mid 2030s and roughly 30% of the mass beyond 2035, tailing into the 2040s. The single most informative variable to watch is not any benchmark score but whether autonomous AI research crosses its threshold before physical and capital constraints flatten the scaling engine — a race that likely resolves by ~2028.

## 1. The instrument problem

Benchmarks are not random samples of cognition. They are adversarially constructed in the gap between what humans and AI can do, built to be "easy for humans, hard for AI" given what AI currently cannot do. This has two consequences.

First, benchmarks die fast. The April 2026 strategic brief by Devresse et al. — whose co-author participated in this synthesis (see disclosure) — proposed an ~8-month benchmark half-life; events since suggest that was conservative for trend-driven benchmarks, a conclusion drawn here from sources independent of that brief. SWE-bench Verified was effectively retired after OpenAI's February 2026 contamination withdrawal. Its successor, SWE-bench Pro, saw its standardized top score move from ~46% (April 2026) to ~59% (Scale standardized public set, July 2026) in roughly ten weeks — while simultaneously fragmenting into three competing "official" numbers (as of early July 2026: 59.1% Scale standardized; 69.2% llm-stats vendor aggregate; 80.3% vendor-reported Claude Fable 5), a decay of *signal* as much as of difficulty.

Second, and less appreciated, the decay is not uniform. It is punctuated. ARC-AGI-1 sat near zero from 2020 through 2023, then collapsed when test-time reasoning (o1/o3) arrived. ARC-AGI-2, launched March 2025, went from ~2.5% to 84.6% (Gemini 3 Deep Think, verified February 2026) in under a year, with Gemini 3.1 Pro at 77.1%; as of July 2026, GPT-5.6 Sol reportedly holds SOTA at 92.5%. ARC-AGI-3, launched March 2026 with frontier systems below 1% (best official at release: 0.37%) against a 100% human solve rate, deliberately built off-path from the current training paradigm — interactive, language-free, scored by squared action-efficiency (RHAE) against human baselines. Its predecessors' history predicts a plateau followed by sudden collapse when a missing paradigm ingredient arrives, not a smooth decline. First movement since release: in July 2026 the ARC Prize Foundation verified GPT-5.6 Sol at 7.8% — a ~20× jump in under four months and the first frontier model to beat a complete game. One verified point does not establish a trajectory, and whether the gain reflects genuine skill-acquisition progress or harness-and-effort scaling is not yet clear from public reporting — the run reportedly used ~2.5× the official compute cap, under which strict scoring would count it at zero[^1]; but it is early evidence *against* a long plateau this cycle, and it moves the April brief's 30–50%-by-EOY-2026 forecast from implausible toward merely ambitious. Averaging that into a "half-life" hides the variable that matters: *when the next ingredient lands.*

[^1]: ARC Prize's own writeup reports 7.78% semi-private / 13.33% public and the first public game won (ft09). The compute figure (~$25k spent against the $10k official cap) comes from public analysis, not the Foundation. The budget question is the live instance of the harness-and-effort caveat — and of the broader point, made operational below, that "hard for AI" is undefined without a resource bound.

**Implication.** Half-life is too noisy and too selection-biased to extrapolate directly. But reframed as a meta-metric — is the half-life staying constant as benchmark territory gets progressively more exotic? how long does it take to build a successor that still discriminates? how contrived must successors become? — it carries information no individual score does. The terminal condition follows naturally: **the criterion for supersession is the failure of benchmark creation itself** — a criterion that, being existential (does *any* discriminating task exist?) rather than sampled, escapes the coverage barrier that defeats ordinary benchmarks. ARC Prize already operates on this philosophy ("as long as there is a gap between AI and human learning, we do not have AGI").

**Three honest limits of the silence criterion.** First, it measures the exhaustion of one direction of gap — parity with human task-design ingenuity — not supersession across all cognition; a gap nobody manages to articulate as a task can survive the silence. Second — and this is a bias, not a blind spot — the surviving gaps are systematically selected. A bounty claim must be adjudicable, adjudication requires affordable verification, and §3's argument is precisely that the residue is selected *for* verification resistance: the gaps most likely to outlive the silence are the gaps least expressible as claims. Reality-graded constructions can reach into that territory, but their verdicts take years, so at the margin that matters the criterion is both biased early and slow to confirm. The silence is therefore a leading indicator of supersession, not a coincident one — and how far it leads depends on a race the document already contains: whether conversion (§3: AI building external verifiers that drain the residue into verifiable territory) outpaces the selection that concentrates it. If conversion wins, the unarticulable remainder at silence-time is small and the criterion runs nearly coincident; if selection wins, the silence can arrive with substantial residue still open. Watch item 4 is the observable face of that race. Third, it has an observability confound: benchmark creation can cease for sociological reasons — funding, attention, liability — rather than capability. The operational form should therefore be *failed, well-resourced attempts*: a standing, well-funded bounty for constructing any discriminating human-easy/AI-hard task — with "hard for AI" pinned to a resource bound (unsolved at, say, the reference human's cost; footnote 1 is the live demonstration that the unpinned version is undefined). This makes the silence a cost-relative, moving target rather than an absolute one, which is less elegant but more honest — and ARC-AGI-3's efficiency scoring against human baselines already concedes the point. The silence is declared when the bounty goes unclaimed — not the mere absence of new benchmarks. Two refinements the criterion needs to survive its own timeline. "Human-easy" must be pinned to a fixed reference population — pre-AI-era expert baselines — because under widespread delegation human skill distributions shift, the gap can close from both sides, and a silence caused by deskilling measures something quite different from supersession; this makes baseline archiving time-critical, since those baselines cannot be collected retroactively once populations deskill (§5, item 7). And the bounty carries Goodhart risk in one direction only: its existential form means training against known task families cannot defeat a genuinely novel construction, but stylistic convergence among human task designers could make the silence arrive spuriously early. ARC Prize's standing competitions already approximate this mechanism, so the marker is less "build this" than "notice when the existing thing goes unclaimed," which strengthens it: the infrastructure predates the incentive to game it. One edge the criterion will eventually force: whether AI-designed tasks count as claims. Near the silence, the strongest task-designers will be the systems under test, and "AI can still construct human-easy/AI-hard tasks that AI cannot solve" is an informative intermediate state — the gap surviving as an object of machine knowledge after it has left human reach — that sits between ordinary progress and the full silence and deserves its own name when it arrives.

## 2. The trend worth extrapolating: task time-horizons

The most principled version of the extrapolation instinct is METR's time-horizon metric: instead of tracking scores on dying tests, measure the *duration* (in expert-human time) of tasks a model completes at a fixed reliability threshold. That horizon has doubled roughly every seven months across model generations, with METR's own analysis suggesting the recent portion of the curve runs faster — closer to four months.

Two properties make this trend load-bearing and four limit it:

- It converts benchmark churn into a dimensioned, comparable quantity, immune to any single benchmark's saturation.
- It already partially internalizes recursive self-improvement: the labs generating the curve were increasingly AI-assisted throughout the measured period. Extrapolating the curve extrapolates that feedback at its historical strength — which cuts both ways. If the feedback strength is growing rather than constant, historical-strength extrapolation systematically *underestimates*; this is precisely why §5 ranks the autonomous-research threshold above any benchmark score.
- It is built almost entirely on verifiable, software-shaped tasks, which biases it optimistic for cognition-at-large (see §3).
- It is threshold-relative. The headline doubling tracks horizons at a 50% success rate; horizons at 80% reliability run several times shorter. "Economically relevant reliability" — this brief's own phrase in §4 — is closer to the higher threshold, so any date extrapolated from the headline curve is the *early* edge of the reliability family, not its center. The dates in §4 track the 50% curve and say so.
- It is, at present, effectively a single-source trend. Treat independent replication of the doubling time as an open item rather than a settled fact; the brief's timeline inherits this dependency.
- It measures capability frontier, not deployment. The 2026 enterprise evidence (Forrester: 25% of planned AI spend postponed to 2027; Gartner: under one-third of decision-makers can identify financial outcomes from AI investments; Bain: "the technology worked, the value didn't arrive"; Uber's $1,500/month agentic tool caps) shows the deployment curve lagging the capability curve by years. The gap between the two curves is itself a measurable, informative quantity.

**Why "one working year" is the chosen threshold.** A working year is approximately the largest unit of work delegated as a single coherent task with defined success criteria (a research project, a product cycle, a dissertation chapter). Longer spans — careers — are compositions of year-scale tasks glued together by problem selection and reputation, and the things that make composition hard for humans (fatigue, memory, mortality) are not AI constraints. That glue — problem selection, long-horizon taste, reputation — is precisely §3's verification-resistant residue: the year-horizon is where task length stops being the story *because* what remains is the residue, not because the glue is easy. Crossing the year-horizon is therefore roughly where task length stops being the binding constraint and verification, trust, and deployment take over. At a 7-month doubling from a mid-2026 frontier of hours-of-expert-work, the 50%-reliability year-horizon lands around 2031–2032; at the faster recent doubling, meaningfully earlier; at an 80%-reliability threshold, later still.

## 3. The task argument: automation is staggered, and the staggering is structural

This is not a caveat to the model; it is part of the model. Three mechanisms:

**Verification asymmetry.** Current training methods devour whatever is cheaply verifiable at scale. Math and code fell first because they grade themselves. Task length is also distributed unevenly across domains — most software tasks are hours to weeks, much of law and medicine is minutes to days per decision, some wet-lab research is years per feedback cycle — so "crossing the one-year horizon" happens in software years before it happens in biology. Any single date quietly averages over this spread.

**Adverse selection of the remainder.** What survives each wave of progress is, by construction, whatever resists cheap verification: judgment whose ground truth arrives years later, taste without rubrics, rare situations, tasks graded only by reality. Nor can the systems' own confidence substitute for the missing ground truth: training objectives reward confident guessing over calibrated abstention (Kalai & Nachum, arXiv:2509.04664), so self-grading is weakest precisely where external verification is absent — the residue cannot certify itself. The escape route is *conversion*, not self-certification: AI can shrink the residue by building external verifiers — formalizing domains, constructing reality-graded feedback loops — that drain verification-resistant territory into verifiable territory; what it cannot do is grade the residue directly. The "last 20% is hardest" framing undersells this — the remainder is not uniformly harder, it is *selected* to be the kind of thing reward signals cannot reach. This is a ratchet: each generation of progress concentrates the residue further. The nearest reference class is self-driving cars, whose first 95% arrived on the extrapolated curve (~2016 confidence, ~2020 predictions) and whose adversely-selected last 5% consumed an additional decade and several companies. It is, however, one reference class, and its neighbors pull in both directions: computer chess's "centaur" era — human-plus-engine superiority, confidently predicted to last decades — collapsed to solo-engine dominance within roughly one; radiology automation ran a decade behind its 2016 predictions without fully arriving; machine translation's 2018 "human parity" claims remain contested on context-heavy work, which is less a data point than a standing warning about parity claims themselves. The spread across these classes, not any single class's informal offset, is the honest error bar on §4's tail.

**Bottom-up measurement is possible and complementary.** Occupation task decompositions (O*NET) crossed with usage data (e.g., Anthropic's Economic Index mapping real Claude usage to occupational tasks) allow tracking the economy-weighted fraction of tasks shifting from augmentation to full delegation, per domain. Through early 2026 that data showed heavy concentration in software and writing, with delegation share climbing as agentic use grows. This yields a *later, economically real* curve; the benchmark/horizon approach yields an *earlier, frontier* curve. Both should be tracked; their divergence is the deployment lag quantified.

Some final strongholds, note, are protected by factors orthogonal to difficulty — embodiment, legal liability, the requirement of an accountable human — and may outlive the cognitive gap entirely, muddying any reading of "human superiority" off what humans still do.

## 4. Timeline of intelligence levels

Central estimates with the reasoning that moves them. Confidence decreases down the table. **All dates are frontier-capability dates, not deployed-economic-reality dates** — add the deployment lag of §2 (years, domain-dependent) to convert. The ordering is intentional: "most economically relevant cognition" can precede the year-horizon crossing because most economy-weighted tasks are short (§3); the year-horizon governs *coherence*, coverage governs *breadth*, and they cross at different times.

*Read the table as scaffolding, not forecast: the probability masses are derived from a thin reference class with informal offsets (§3) and are stated in order to be updated against §5's watch items — see the status note before quoting any of them.*

| Stage | Criterion | Central estimate | Key drivers / risks |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Verifiable-domain superhumanity** | Superhuman at most cheaply-verifiable cognitive work (math, code, structured analysis) at economically relevant reliability | **~2027–2029** | Already partially realized; driven by RL on verifiable rewards. Risk: reliability at long task lengths, not peak skill. |
| **Autonomous AI research threshold** | AI contribution to AI R&D crosses from acceleration (~tens of %) to running the loop | **Watch by ~2028** | *The* pivotal variable. Race between self-improvement feedback and compute/capital constraints (fab, power, construction timelines; post-reckoning capex discipline). |
| **Year-horizon crossing** | Completion of coherent tasks taking a skilled human ~1 working year, at METR's 50%-success threshold (verifiable domains first) | **~2029–2032** | 7-month doubling → ~2031–32; 4-month recent rate → earlier. Threshold-relative: at 80% reliability — closer to "economically relevant" — the crossing lands later (§2). Domain-staggered per §3. |
| **Most economically relevant cognition** | Superhuman on the bulk of economy-weighted cognitive tasks, incl. significant scientific contribution | **~2029–2031** (central mass) | Major AI-driven discoveries expected to *precede* the silence by years. Lag driver: verification-resistant domains, deployment gap. |
| **The silence** | The cost-bounded bounty of §1 goes unclaimed: no well-funded designer can construct any remaining human-easy (pre-AI baseline) / AI-hard (at reference-human cost) task | **~10% by 2029; mode 2031–2034 (~55–60% of mass); beyond 2035: ~30%, tail into the 2040s** | Trailing edge by construction — though a *leading* indicator of true supersession, biased early by the selection mechanism of §1's second limit. Tail mechanisms: capital/physical flattening of the scaling engine; adverse selection of the remainder (ARC-AGI-3-type fluid skill acquisition twice showed a plateau-then-collapse pattern — though v3's early movement (§1) already weighs against a long plateau this cycle). The disanalogies from self-driving's last-5% decade (§3) — cognitive benchmarks don't demand six-nines reliability, capability gains transfer across strongholds where driving progress didn't, and uniquely the system under test attacks its own residue by conversion (§3), not self-grading — offset the reference class partially, not fully; the ~30% beyond 2035 is its residual claim after those offsets. Neighboring classes bracket it: the chess-centaur collapse argues faster, radiology argues slower (§3). What moves the number: evidence the disanalogies fully offset argues toward ~15%; taking driving's residue as the true prior argues toward ~45%. |

The endpoint will likely be blurry in prospect and punctuated in retrospect. Candidate retrospective markers: the first major discovery where the human role was "asked and checked"; a flagship benchmark saturating before its paper clears review; a foundation declining to build a successor benchmark because none would discriminate; and — the quietest tell — new evaluations ceasing to report human baselines because collecting them stopped being worth the cost.

## 5. What to watch, in order of information value

1. **The 2028 race**: autonomous-research threshold vs. capex/compute flattening. Resolves more uncertainty than any benchmark.
2. **METR doubling time**: whether the recent ~4-month rate holds, reverts to ~7, or lengthens — tracked at a stated reliability threshold, since the 50% and 80% curves answer different questions (§2).
3. **ARC-AGI-3 trajectory shape**: the first watch item to fire — verified movement from 0.37% (March 2026) to 7.8% (July 2026, GPT-5.6 Sol) argues against plateau persistence, with the compute-budget dispute (§1, footnote 1) still open. Next markers: whether the trajectory continues toward the 30–50% EOY range, whether progress comes from within-paradigm scaling or a nameable new ingredient, and whether verified runs land within the official resource cap. Squared efficiency scoring still understates absolute progress.
4. **Successor-benchmark design cost**: time-to-discriminating-successor after each saturation, and how contrived successors become. This is also the observable face of the conversion-vs-selection race in §1: falling design cost in formerly verification-resistant territory means conversion is winning; rising contrivance with stable residue means selection is.
5. **Delegation share in usage data**: economy-weighted augmentation→delegation shift, per domain (bottom-up curve).
6. **The capability–deployment gap**: whether the enterprise ROI discipline of 2026 compresses or extends the lag.
7. **Pre-AI expert baselines (the action item)**: the reference population that defines "human-easy" (§1) cannot be reconstructed after widespread deskilling — once delegation shifts human skill distributions, the baseline is gone. Archiving broad, well-documented expert baselines now — task performances with time-and-reliability profiles, not just scores — is the one entry on this list that is an action rather than an observation, and the only one with a closing window.
